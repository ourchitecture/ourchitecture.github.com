High Fidelity Understanding and Alignment
"Understanding one another can be difficult; understanding complex organizations with layers of communications is much harder."

Vertical alignment ensures that associates and their leadership understand one another.

Horizontal alignment ensures that associates, their teams, and their organizational structures understand one another across the organization. This includes teams, product groups, initiatives and programs, communities, departments, divisions, and business units.

Fundamentally, the business objectives must align; this includes intentional, cooperative competition or "coopetition" within the organization.

Technology objectives, in support of the business, must also align.

Organizational Foundations

Mission, Vision and Values

The traditional trio of elements that describe an organization's purpose, the ideal future state that the organization hopes to achieve, and the core principles that guide and direct the organization.

Why?

Expanding on the traditional "Mission" statement, Simon Sinek's book series and lectures on "Start with why" encouraged organizations to more effectively communicate the organization's purpose and to let the "Why" inform "How" the organization works to fulfill its purpose and the "What".

Business Model Canvas

The Strategyzer organization drastically simplified the overall topology of capturing and communicating an organization's business model with the "Business Model Canvas".

Business Capability Models and Maps

It is critical that an organization can clearly communicate the capabilities of the organization and how those capabilities provide value out to customers, through partners, in to associates, and out to community.

"... capability models focus on helping an organization continually improve and progress, realizing that the technological and business landscape is ever-changing." (Forsgren, Nicole, et al. Accelerate. IT Revolution Press, 2018)
The Four Organizational Elements

The traditional triangle of People, Process, and Technology has been adapted with a focus on why they exist in the first place; the Value they create and support. The order of these four elements was carefully chosen to focus on People-first and the Value they work to provide, supported by Technology and Process.

People

The most important assets to any organization are the people that power it.

Value

While companies offer products and services, the value that an organization ultimately creates is much more diverse.

Technology

In addition to digital experiences for customers, many organizations now offer digital value too.

Process

The Value stream, supported by the People and the digital supply chain of Technology.

Assets

The organizational assets that hold and provide value.
